● Yes — Clarke's central move (building on Dewey and Strauss) is:

  The situation of action/inquiry IS the unit of analysis. Not actors, not processes, not social worlds — the situation itself.

  Key formal properties:

  1. No outside. "There is no such thing as context." What traditional research calls "context" (institutions, discourses, politics,
  history) is constitutive of the situation, not external framing. Everything relevant is IN the situation.
  2. Relational constitution. Elements don't pre-exist the situation and then enter it — they are constituted through their relations
  within it. (This is where Clarke is most explicitly Dewey/Bentley-transactional.)
  3. Heterogeneous composition. The situation contains human actors, nonhuman actants, discursive constructions, political-economic
  elements, temporal elements, spatial elements, major issues/debates — all on the same ontological footing.
  4. Empirical determination. The situation is not given a priori — it must be analytically articulated. The situational map IS the
  method for this: "What is in this situation? What are the relations among them?"
  5. The conditional IS the situational. Clarke's explicit break from Strauss's conditional matrix — conditions aren't layers around
  the situation, they're elements within it.

  The Sit Map then is the first-order analytical instrument: lay out ALL elements, then systematically consider every pairwise
  relation. No hierarchy, no grouping — flat relational space first.

  Is this what you had in mind, or were you looking for a specific passage/formulation? And — are we thinking about how this maps onto
  the current Sit Map implementation?

  - Strauss: concentric layers (micro → macro) — implies hierarchy and an inside/outside distinction
  - Clarke: everything on one plane, inside the circle. The dashed outer boundary = permeable, not fixed. No layering.

● Good question. Clarke's value claim for the ordered version is essentially pragmatic-heuristic, not analytical:

  1. Comprehensiveness check. The primary function: "Have I considered X?" Researchers default to human actors. The categories are a
  corrective — they force attention to nonhuman elements, discourses, spatial/temporal aspects that interactionist analysis typically
  ignores. Without prompts, the messy map tends to reproduce the researcher's existing gaze.

  2. The discursive split. The most analytically productive distinction: separating "actors" from "discursive constructions of actors."
   "Patients" as people who act vs. "the patient" as a discursive figure (how patients are talked about, governed, imagined). This is
  Clarke's Foucauldian extension of interactionism — it doesn't emerge naturally from most researchers' first pass.

  3. Implicated/silent actors. Having this as an explicit category is a power-analysis forcing function. Without it, silences remain
  invisible.

  4. Transition device. The ordered version is NOT the analytical product — it's a sorting step that prepares for the actual work:
  systematic pairwise relational interrogation. "Now that I see what I have, let me check every pair."

  But — and I think this is where your skepticism lands — there's a real tension:

  - The messy map is epistemically honest: flat, no hierarchy, pure empirical articulation
  - The ordered version introduces pre-given categories from Strauss that could impose structure the data doesn't warrant
  - From a strict D/B transactional standpoint, pre-sorting IS a perspectival act that prematurely collapses superpositions — an
  element that is simultaneously "nonhuman" AND "discursive construction" gets forced into one box
  - Clarke herself acknowledges this by saying "no absolute need" and "you may have other categories" — which raises the question: if
  they're this optional, what exactly is the epistemological status?

  My reading: Clarke values them as scaffolding for the novice analyst — training wheels that ensure you look beyond actors. For an
  experienced researcher working within the transactional ontology, the messy map + systematic relational interrogation might be
  sufficient, and the ordered version risks doing more harm (premature categorization) than good.

  For transact-qda: does this reinforce your instinct to NOT build these categories into the tool as structural features?
