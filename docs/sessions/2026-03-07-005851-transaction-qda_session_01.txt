  Key for Our Project

  The productive question you raised — whether D/B's naming forms help us
  identify Prozessierungsformen for our data objects — I think the answer is:
  partially yes, but with a crucial extension.

  What D/B give us is a processual gradient of determination: Cue →
  Characterization → Specification. This maps well onto a research process:
  something first appears as a vague signal (cue), gets provisionally named
  (characterization), and is progressively specified through inquiry. Every
  naming in our system could carry a marker of where it sits on this gradient.

  But — and this connects to your point about destructive interference — D/B's
  gradient is monotonically toward more determination. It doesn't account for
  the reverse movement: the deliberate de-specification, the moment when
  something well-determined dissolves back into superposition, or when a silence
   is registered. The Barad-point: that the zero of interference is itself a
  positive finding.

  So I'd propose: D/B's Cue → Characterization → Specification gives us one axis
   of processuality. But we need a second axis that D/B don't provide —
  something like resolution/dissolution or collapse/superposition — to capture
  the symmetry you described between presence and absence, between the visible
  and the concretely-not-there.

  Shall we sketch how this maps onto the data model concretely? For instance,
  should appearances carry a field indicating their designational stage
  (cue/characterization/specification)?

  Other Notable Entries

  - Behavior: "Always transactionally: never as of the organism alone... but
  always of the organic-environmental situation, with organisms and
  environmental objects taken as equally its aspects."
  - Circularity: "Regarded as a radical defect by non-transactional
  epistemologies... Normal for inquiry into knowings and knowns in system."
  - Context: "Where it obscures the issues of naming and the named... more apt
  to do harm than good."
  - Description: "Cues organizing characterizations; characterizations
  developing into specifications... A name is, in effect, a truncated
  description."
  - Observation: "Durationally and extensionally transactional, and thus neither
   separately in terms of the observing, nor separately of the observed."
  - Inquiry: "A strictly transactional name. Equivalent of knowing."
  - Thing: "Most generally used for anything named. Its very generality gives it
   frequent advantage over Entity and Substance."

  ---
  What strikes me reading this systematically: D/B's vocabulary is almost
  entirely oriented toward the positive determination of the named — the
  gradient from vague to firm. There is no entry for silence, absence,
  withdrawal, or non-appearance. The closest is their insistence on openness and
   tentativeness — that specification never reaches finality — but that's
  epistemological modesty, not an ontological account of productive absence.

  Their Aspect and Phase are interesting for your purposes though: aspects are
  "not independents" (components of a transactional situation), and phases are
  aspects with "definite spatial and temporal localizations." This maps directly
   onto our appearances model — an appearance IS a phase in D/B's sense.

  And their distinction between System (transactional) vs. Organization
  (interactional) could be productive for differentiating map types or
  analytical perspectives.
